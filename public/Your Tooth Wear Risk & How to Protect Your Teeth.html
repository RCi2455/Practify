Your Tooth Wear Risk & How to Protect Your Teeth

Personalised guidance following your dental consultation  ·  For patients aged 18–98

A small amount of tooth wear is perfectly normal as we get older. What we're looking at here is wear that goes beyond what's expected for your age — and understanding why it's happening is the first step to slowing it down or stopping it altogether.

The most common causes of this kind of wear are:

  • Frequent acidic food or drink
  • Stomach acid from reflux or vomiting
  • Tooth grinding, often at night without knowing it
  • Certain acidic medications

The good news is that with a few targeted changes, we can often make a real difference. This leaflet explains your personal risk level and what you can do about it.

Your BEWE risk category

Your risk level is based on your Basic Erosive Wear Examination (BEWE) — a standardised way of measuring tooth wear — alongside your personal risk factors. Your dentist will have discussed which category applies to you.

Low Risk
BEWE score 0–2

You have very minor signs of tooth wear — keep doing what works! A few simple habits will keep things that way.

  • Use a fluoride toothpaste twice daily (e.g. Colgate Total, Sensodyne Daily Care)
  • Limit acidic drinks like juice, fizzy drinks, or wine — keep them to mealtimes
  • Sip water regularly, especially after acidic food or drink
  • Brush gently with a soft toothbrush
Moderate Risk
BEWE score 3–8

There are early signs of enamel wear. Now is the time to protect your enamel more actively — small changes now can prevent bigger problems later.

  • Avoid sipping acidic drinks throughout the day — stick to mealtimes
  • Rinse with plain water or a fluoride mouthwash after acidic foods and drinks
  • Use a high-fluoride toothpaste (Duraphat 2800ppm or 5000ppm, if prescribed)
  • Wait 30–60 minutes after acidic food or drink before brushing
High Risk
BEWE score 9–13

Tooth wear is becoming more noticeable. Active prevention is essential — and we'll work with you to put a plan in place.

  • Keep acidic food and drink exposures to fewer than 4 times per day
  • Drink acidic drinks quickly, ideally through a straw
  • Use a high-fluoride toothpaste or prescription-strength mouthwash
  • If you grind your teeth, talk to us about a custom nightguard
  • Let us know if you experience heartburn, reflux, or regular tooth sensitivity
Very High Risk
BEWE score 14+

Significant wear is present. We'll support you with a treatment and prevention plan tailored to your situation — you don't have to manage this alone.

  • Restorative treatment may be needed to protect your worn teeth
  • We'll guide you through adjusting diet and habits to reduce acid exposure
  • A protective bite guard can help if night-time grinding is a factor
  • If reflux or vomiting is contributing, we may recommend medical support alongside dental care
  • Fluoride treatments such as varnish or gel may be applied in-clinic

Extra guidance for your specific risk factors

Depending on what's driving your tooth wear, some of the following sections will be particularly relevant to you. Your dentist will have highlighted the most important ones.

Acidic diet

  • Reduce how often you have acidic items — fizzy drinks, juice, citrus, vinegar-based sauces, and smoothies are the main culprits
  • Keep acidic foods and drinks to mealtimes and drink water afterwards
  • Try less acidic alternatives: milk, herbal teas, or still water are all gentle on enamel
  • Sugar-free chewing gum after meals can help neutralise acid — look for gum containing xylitol

Acid reflux or vomiting

  • After an episode of reflux or vomiting, rinse with water or fluoride mouthwash — do not brush straight away
  • Speak to your GP about managing reflux, particularly if it happens at night
  • A higher-concentration fluoride toothpaste is strongly recommended — ask us about a prescription
  • Sleeping with your head slightly elevated can help reduce night-time acid exposure

Tooth grinding (bruxism)

  • Grinding often happens during sleep and many people are unaware of it
  • A custom-fitted nightguard (occlusal splint) can protect your teeth while you sleep — ask us about this
  • Stress management techniques and jaw exercises can help reduce daytime clenching
  • Avoid chewing gum or hard foods excessively — these can aggravate jaw tension

Acidic medications

  • Includes vitamin C tablets, aspirin, iron supplements, and some liquid medicines
  • Swallow tablets quickly with water — do not suck or chew them unless specifically instructed to
  • Rinse your mouth with water afterwards
  • Speak to your doctor or pharmacist about alternatives if medication is affecting your teeth
  • A fluoride toothpaste or mouthwash (e.g. Fluorigard, Duraphat) can help protect enamel

One final thought

Small, consistent daily habits make the biggest difference with tooth wear. It's not about being perfect — it's about making slightly better choices more often. Come back and see us regularly, bring any questions, and we'll track how things are going together. Your teeth are built to last a lifetime with the right support.
