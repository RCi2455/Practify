Bisphosphonates & Your Dental Health

What you need to know before starting treatment

If you've been prescribed bisphosphonates — whether for osteoporosis, bone thinning, or another condition — this leaflet is here to help you understand what this means for your dental health.

The good news is that with the right care and a little extra attention, most people on bisphosphonates go through treatment with no dental problems at all. We're here to help make sure that's the case for you.

What are bisphosphonates?

Bisphosphonates are medicines that help strengthen bones and slow down bone loss. They're commonly prescribed for osteoporosis, Paget's disease, and sometimes as part of cancer treatment. You may know them by brand names such as Alendronic Acid, Risedronate, Zoledronic Acid, or Denosumab.

They work by reducing the activity of cells that break down bone tissue, which helps bones stay stronger for longer.

Why does this affect my dental care?

Bisphosphonates affect the blood supply and healing capacity of the jawbone. In rare cases, dental procedures that disturb the gum or bone — particularly tooth extractions — can lead to a condition called osteonecrosis of the jaw (ONJ), where a small area of jaw bone doesn't heal as it should.

Important to know

ONJ is uncommon, especially with oral bisphosphonates taken as tablets. The risk is higher with intravenous (IV) bisphosphonates used in cancer treatment. Whatever your situation, the best protection is keeping your mouth as healthy as possible so that extractions are less likely to be needed in the first place.

The most important thing: keep your teeth

The single most important thing you can do is look after your natural teeth so they never need to be removed. Losing a tooth while on bisphosphonates carries a higher risk than it would otherwise — so prevention is everything.

  • Brush twice a day with a fluoride toothpaste — morning and last thing at night
  • Clean between your teeth daily using floss or interdental brushes
  • Attend regular dental check-ups, at least every six months
  • Book a hygienist appointment every six months to keep gum disease at bay
  • Tell us straight away if a tooth becomes painful, loose, or sensitive — don't wait
  • Avoid smoking, which significantly increases the risk of gum disease and poor healing

Before you start bisphosphonates

Ideally, we'd love to see you before you begin treatment. If there are any teeth that may need to come out in the future — due to decay, gum disease, or other reasons — it's much safer to deal with these before bisphosphonates are started.

Already started treatment? Don't worry

If you're already taking bisphosphonates, it's still very much worth coming in. We can assess your dental health, address any concerns, and put a prevention plan in place to keep things stable throughout your treatment.

What about routine dental treatment?

Usually fine to go ahead

Check-ups, fillings, crowns, root canal treatment, and hygiene appointments carry no increased risk and should continue as normal.

Requires extra care

Tooth extractions and implants need careful planning. We'll discuss this with you and, if needed, liaise with your medical team before proceeding.

We will always discuss your medication with you before any treatment and plan together the safest approach. You should never feel rushed or uncertain — please ask us anything.

Tell us about all your medications

It's really important that we have an up-to-date list of everything you're taking, including your bisphosphonate, how long you've been on it, and whether it's taken as a tablet or given by infusion. Please bring your medication list or a repeat prescription slip to every appointment.

Please contact us promptly if you notice

Any pain, swelling, numbness, or exposed bone in your mouth — especially if it doesn't settle within a week or two. These symptoms are rare but we want to hear from you quickly if they occur.

A final word of reassurance

We understand that receiving a new diagnosis and starting long-term medication can feel daunting. Please know that bisphosphonate-related dental complications, while real, are uncommon — and with good oral hygiene and regular care, most patients have no problems at all.

We're your partners in this. Come and see us regularly, keep your mouth clean, and don't hesitate to get in touch if you have any concerns at all.
